At the heart of the debate related to our forests lies the fact that the forest resources are limited and are subject to competing needs. We expect forests to supply us raw materials, sequester CO2, safeguard biodiversity and adapt to the climate crisis. This generates conflicts in politics and society.

To be able to address this issue commensurating is needed. To make something commensurate means it can be measured by a common standard. A group of researchers studied three different strategies concerning the forests of Finland. They wanted to see whether they are compatible, so they estimated what kind of forest use is needed for each.

It turned out that they are conflicting. Not one of them can be reached with our current forest use. However well we calculate possible solutions and models, what it all comes down to in the end is values and choices. Do we think biodiversity is valuable? How much of it do we want to save? Do we only save what’s beneficial for humanity’s wellbeing, or do we have a duty to do more? A dialogue is needed, and after that political coordination is the key.
